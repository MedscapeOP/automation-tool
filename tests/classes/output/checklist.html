Stigma and Quality of Life in Patients With Frequent Migraine

<<insert slide 2>>

CaMEO Study: Background[1]

  • Nearly 20,000 respondents invited to complete Family Burden Module
    • Asked respondents to postulate how their lives would be different if they did not have migraine

<<insert slide 3>>

CaMEO Study: Patient Demographics[2]

<<insert slide 4>>

CaMEO Study: Effect of Migraine on Life if I Didn't Have Migraine[2]

  • Most patients with chronic migraine (CM) and episodic migraine (EM) indicated overall health, stress levels, and enjoyment of free time would be better or a lot better without migraine
  • Across 9 domains assessed, almost 90% of respondents with CM and 70% with EM said lives would be better or a lot better in at least 1 area without migraine

<<insert slide 5>>

CaMEO Study: Effect of Migraine on Personal Career and Finances[3]

  • Second analysis focusing on the perceived impact of migraine on career and finances
  • Increasing headache day frequency in EM and CM groups associated with higher rates of concern

<<insert slide 6>>

CaMEO Study: Effect of Migraine on Partner's Career and Finances[3]

  • Respondents reported spouse or partner career adversely affected career advancement
    • Missed work, changing jobs, or leaving or passing up a job
    • Those with CM twice as likely vs EM to agree somewhat or completely

<<insert slide 7>>

CaMEO Study: Effect of Migraine on Relationships[4]

  • Third analysis examined perceived effect of migraine on family activities, relationships, emotional reactions, and major life decisions
    • 65% of respondents in an intimate relationship such as marriage or domestic partnership or were living together
    • 10% in a committed relationship but not living together
    • About one-quarter not currently involved in an intimate relationship
    • Subset of questions about parenting and effect on children for those with children living in the home
  • Approximately 20% of those not in a relationship or in a relationship and not living together indicated headaches contributed to relationship problems in the past
  • Increasing headache day frequency was associated with increased rates of endorsement that migraine or headaches cause problems
    • Held true for all areas of relationships

Considerations in Preventive Therapy for Migraine

<<insert slide 2>>

Patterns of Preventive Therapy Use: IBMS-II[5]

  • After starting on oral generic preventive medications for migraine
    • After 6 months, only 25% remain on treatment
    • After a year, only 15% remain on treatment
  • If ask people why they discontinue, they give 2 major reasons independent of what class of medication they are on
    • The medicine does not work well enough
    • The medicine causes too many side effects

<<insert slide 3>>

Weighing Treatment Efficacy and Tolerability

  • When thinking about whether to continue a patient on preventive treatment, look at 2 × 2 table of efficacy vs side effects
    • In cell A, the treatment is working, does not produce side effects
    • Very likely the treatment that should be continued
  • In cell D, the treatment is not effective and produces side effects
    • Very little chance patients will continue treatment and clinicians also unlikely to continue
  • Cell C is the most common situation with oral generic preventive medications for migraine
    • Medication is at least somewhat effective but also producing side effects
    • Patients and clinicians have a difficult choice to make
  • Emerging monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) for migraine are particularly attractive
    • They provide efficacy with little in the way of penalty in terms of adverse events
    • Much greater chance of keeping people in cell A

<<insert slide 4>>

Measuring Migraine Burden in All Phases[6,7]

  • Phases of migraine
    • In the preheadache (premonitory/prodrome) phase, people may have symptoms or auras
    • In the postheadache (postdrome) phase, people may have changes in mood or behavior, fatigue, and scalp sensitivity

<<insert slide 5>>

Measuring Burden of Migraine Before, During, and After Migraine: MPFID[8,9]

  • New study data on measuring the burden of migraine during the headache phase, and on the day before and the day after headache
  • Migraine Physical Function Impact Diary (MPFID) used to measure disability on headache days and nonheadache days
    • Daily diary measure, asks 13 questions about ability to function

<<insert slide 6>>

Burden on Everyday Activities and Physical Impairment by Migraine Phase[9]

  • MPFID shows burden on headache days is much greater than on days where no headache is present
  • Substantial burden on prodromal and postdromal days
    • Previously unappreciated in migraine studies
  • In future studies, measure the burden of migraine and treatment benefits on headache days, prodromal days, and postdromal days
    • Get a fuller picture both of the burden of disease and the benefits of treatment

<<insert slide 7>>

Identifying Natural Subgroups of Migraine[10]

  • Study identifying natural subgroups of migraine to address questions
    • Why is migraine so common?
    • Why do the clinical features of migraine vary so much from person to person?
    • Why is treatment response so variable?
      • Why does one person respond to a tricyclic antidepressant, another to a beta-blocker, and another to an antiepilepsy drug?
    • Why has it been so difficult to identify genes for migraine?
      • Identified genes for rare forms of migraine like familial hemiplegic migraine
    • Most of genetic determinants of more common forms of migraine remain to be identified
  • Solution is to do a better job of characterizing natural subgroups of people with migraine
    • One approach is to use comorbidity profiles
      • Use conditions that travel along with migraine to identify natural subgroups of migraine

<<insert slide 8>>

Natural Subgroups of Migraine: Overall Results[10]

  • Examined people with migraine and 22 comorbidities
    • Discovered 8 natural subgroups

<<insert slide 9>>

Natural Subtype of Migraine: Likelihood of Progression to CM[10]

  • Subgroups differed widely in prognosis
    • Group with the most comorbidities was most likely to progress to CM
    • Group with the fewest comorbidities was least likely to progress
    • Substantial differences among groups in prognosis

<<insert slide 10>>

Looking Ahead

  • Open questions
    • Will these subgroups predict response to treatment?
    • Will they correlate with neuroimaging abnormalities found in migraine?
  • Important clinical question
    • Instead of engaging in a trial-and-error-process for treatment, might be able to get treatment right the first time

Migraine Headache: A Treatment Update

<<insert slide 2>>

Lasmiditan for Acute Treatment of Migraine[11,12]

  • Two recent updates in acute treatment of migraine
  • New 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT)1F receptor agonist lasmiditan
    • Built on the platform of triptans, which are 5-HT1B, 1D, and to some extent, 5-HT1F receptor agonists
    • Because of the 1D receptor agonism, triptans are contraindicated in patients with cardiovascular disease or significant risk factors for cardiovascular disease
  • Lasmiditan removed the 1B and the 1D agonism and just left the 1F receptor agonism
    • 1F receptors are not located on blood vessels, so there is no cardiovascular liability associated with this medication
  • Results of 2 large studies reported: SAMURAI and SPARTAN
    • Both large, placebo-controlled, randomized, double-blind studies evaluating efficacy and safety of lasmiditan for acute treatment of migraine

<<insert slide 3>>

Lasmiditan: Phase 3 Study Results[12]

  • Used by patients to treat moderate or severe attacks
  • Primary endpoint was percentage of patients pain free at 2 hours and percentage of patients free of their most bothersome symptom at 2 hours
    • In both studies, 28% to 38% of patients were pain free at 2 hours
    • About two-thirds had relief of headache in 2 hours
    • More than 40% had relief of the most bothersome symptom
  • Both studies showed that all 3 doses tested -- 50, 100, 200 mg -- were all highly statistically superior to placebo
  • No serious safety signals
    • Dizziness the most common adverse event, reported in about 16% to 18% of patients
  • Lasmiditan, if approved, will provide an option for patients for whom triptans are contraindicated
    • Option also for patients for whom triptans were not effective or were not tolerated

<<insert slide 4>>

Rimegepant for Acute Treatment of Migraine: Phase 3 Study Results[13,14]

  • Studies looked at efficacy of 2 calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)-receptor antagonists
    • CGRP is an important neuropeptide in the pathogenesis of migraine
  • CGRP receptor antagonists rimegepant and ubrogepant evaluated in large randomized placebo-controlled studies
    • Two studies each, or 4 studies in total
  • Both CGRP receptor antagonists were shown to be superior to placebo
    • Pain-free rates of about 20% for both molecules
    • About two-thirds of patients achieved relief of headache at 2 hours

<<insert slide 5>>

Ubrogepant for Acute Treatment of Migraine: Phase 3 Study Results[15,16]

  • Percentage of patients able to return to normal function exceeded 40% in studies evaluating these CGRP receptor antagonists
  • Like lasmiditan, CGRP receptor antagonists do not constrict blood vessels
    • Should have no contraindication for patients with established cardiovascular disease or significant risk factors for cardiovascular disease
  • No serious safety signals seen with either drugs in any of the 4 studies
    • No serious side effects
    • Side effect profile for both compounds look very similar and comparable to placebo

<<insert slide 6>>

CGRP-Targeting mAbs for Migraine Prevention[6,17-26]

  • Erenumab, a CGRP-targeting mAb, has been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for prevention of migraine
  • Two drugs are under FDA review right now and a third, an intravenous formulation, is completing phase 3 trials
  • All of these mAbs are effective for the preventive treatment of episodic migraine and chronic migraine

<<insert slide 7>>

Early Rapid Onset of CGRP-Targeting mAbs[27-29]

  • Effective in reducing the frequency of migraine
  • Rapid onset of effect, within about a day or a week
    • Unprecedented, for preventive treatments for migraine

<<insert slide 8>>

Benefit of CGRP-Targeting mAbs in Difficult-to-Treat Populations[30-35]

  • Effective in patients who were otherwise medically or treatment resistant
  • Effective in patients who have failed multiple prophylactic medications
  • Effective in patients with medication overuse headache, independent of withdrawal from acute analgesics
  • Unique attributes that go far above and beyond just their efficacy in preventing or reducing the frequency of migraine

Emerging Approaches for Preventive Therapy of CM

<<insert slide 2>>

CM: Features and Challenges[6,36,37]

  • CM and EM are clinically, functionally, and anatomically differentiated
    • Evidence suggests they may be separate conditions
  • Patients with CM usually have more comorbid conditions and more frequent medication overuse
    • Complicates clinical management of CM
  • Estimated rate of reversion from CM to EM is relatively low
    • Studies report remission rates of about 15% to 26%
  • Several mAbs targeting CGRP are in development
    • The first was recently approved for migraine prevention

<<insert slide 3>>

Fremanezumab for Prevention of CM: HALO-CM[22]

  • Study looked at reversion of patients with CM to EM with fremanezumab treatment
  • Fremanezumab is a CGRP mAb that can be given as a monthly or quarterly injection for prevention of migraine
    • In clinical trials, shown to significantly reduce frequency of migraine and headache without serious treatment-related adverse events
  • Phase 3 HALO-CM study
    • Patients aged 18 to 70 years with history of migraine for at least 12 months and who met International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD)-3 criteria for CM
    • Randomly assigned to fremanezumab given monthly or quarterly or placebo
    • Patients kept a daily diary and completed other questionnaires at regular intervals
  • Primary endpoint was mean change in the monthly average number of headache days of at least moderate severity at 12 weeks vs baseline
    • Secondary endpoints included safety and tolerability measures
  • Study met all of its endpoints

<<insert slide 4>>

Reversion From CM to EM With Fremanezumab in HALO-CM[38]

  • Post hoc analysis of HALO-CM data looked at proportion of patients who reverted from CM to EM
    • Met primary outcome
  • Fremanezumab demonstrated efficacy as a preventive treatment for CM
    • Decrease in mean MHD over the treatment period
  • Demonstrated potential benefit for reversion from CM to EM

<<insert slide 5>>

Eptinezumab for Prevention of CM: PROMISE-2[21]

  • PROMISE-2 study of the CGRP-targeting mAb eptinezumab as a preventive therapy for CM
    • Randomly assigned to a single quarterly infusion of 100 mg or 300 mg dose of eptinezumab or placebo
    • Enrolled more than 1000 patients who had approximately 16 monthly migraine days at baseline
  • Subjects in both eptinezumab groups had a greater reduction in monthly migraine days vs placebo
  • Study also met secondary endpoints
    • More than half of subjects achieved >50% reduction in MMD from weeks 1 to 12 and at monthly intervals
    • Significantly higher proportion of patients on eptinezumab achieved 100% reduction in monthly migraine days vs placebo
