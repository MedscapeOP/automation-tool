<<insert slide 4 (02:38) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>>Cancer-Associated Thrombosis: How to Identify Those at Risk [1]

• Surveys suggest that cancer patients are very unware of the risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE)

• They are very aware of becoming nauseated, having emesis, and getting infections and when to report these problems

• They are not aware of leg swelling or increased shortness of breath or sudden onset of chest pain as being concerning

• Providers must assess the risk, have a discussion, and educate patients about warning signs and symptoms of VTE

<<level 2>> Study Results[5,6]

• In both those trials, the non-vitamin K antagonist oral anticoagulants (NOACs) were better in terms of reducing the risk of recurrent VTE with some increase in bleeding

• Both trials assessed the 6-month time point

o The Hokusai study extended to 12 months, but for most patients the median time was around 6 months

<<insert slide 12 (09:24) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>>LMWH and VKAs[7]

• The advantage of low-molecular heparin vs vitamin K antagonist (VKA) is that you can titrate the dose

• If, for example, a patient presents with gastrointestinal bleeding (GI), a lower dose can be used to start and then the dose can be gradually increased when bleeding ceases

• Or, if a patient develops thrombocytopenia, you can titrate the dose down and ramp it back up again as the platelet count recovers

• There is a flexibility in dosing, which is not seen with VKAs

o VKAs are only effective when the international normalized ratio (INR) is between 2 and 3, and that is difficult to maintain

<<insert slide 13 (10:57) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>>Study Results (cont)[5,6]

• Hokusai VTE Cancer and SELECT-D studies had similar results

• Bleeding was seen especially in patients with GI cancer

• There are now broader options for anticoagulation to manage thrombosis in our cancer patients

<<insert slide 14 (13:00) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>> Patient Persistence with Anticoagulant: LMWH vs Oral Agents[8]

• Consistently in studies looking at different populations -- older patients, younger patients, patients with private insurance, patients with Medicare -- the results are the same

o Persistence on low molecular weight heparin (LMWH) is much lower than persistence on any oral agent, including VKAs

• There is clearly a limitation of prolonged parenteral therapy that needs to be borne in mind

<<insert slide 15 (14:28) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>> Challenges of Anticoagulation Therapy

• In the Hokusai VTE Cancer study, for example, only about 11% of the patients had hematological malignancies; thrombocytopenia was not a big issue

• In patents with lymphoma or leukemia, thrombocytopenia becomes a major challenge

• If anticoagulants are stopped, the risk of reoccurrence is at least around 20% in that first year[9]

<<insert slide 16 (18:21) chapter title: Ottawa Score>>

<<level 2>> Ottawa Risk Score[10]

• The Ottawa score has not been validated in every study

• It is very good at predicting low-risk patients, but not so good at predicting high-risk patients

• The other challenge is all of these risk assessment tools were developed in the LMWH era
