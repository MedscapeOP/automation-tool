Question type: CME Posttest

Question Introduction (optional ι):

Question 1: Which of the following best describes the burden of migraine on career, finances, and relationships?

Answer choices ι:

• The impact on relationships is similar among those with chronic migraine (CM) and those with episodic migraine (EM)

• The impact on partner career is minimal and independent of whether CM or EM is present

• The impact on personal and partner career and finances, as well as on relationships, is substantial and is greater in those with CM vs EM

• The impact on personal career and finances, as well as on relationships, is moderate, and does not affect overall health and well-being

Answer explanation: Data from the Chronic Migraine Epidemiology and Outcomes (CaMEO) study show a consistent belief among patients with migraine that it has a detrimental effect on many aspects of life, including relationships, family life, career, finances, and overall health. Across all domains, those with CM were 2 to 3 times more likely to express negative effects due to migraine vs those with EM. Among those with EM, the extent to which headaches were perceived to be a problem increased with headache day frequency.

Question type: CME Posttest

Question Introduction (optional ι): A 32-year-old man with CM is treated with the CGRP (calcitonin gene-related peptide) monoclonal antibody (mAb) fremanezumab.

Question 2: Based on clinical trial data, which of following is the most likely outcome?

Answer choices ι:

• Reduction in headache and migraine days per month and possible reversion to EM

• Reduction in headache days per month and reduction in impaired functioning on preheadache days

• Reduction in migraine days per month and reduction in cardiovascular comorbidities

• Reduction in headache and migraine days per month, improved functioning on pre- and postheadache days, and improved comorbidity profiles

Answer explanation: Results of the HALO-CM trial of fremanezumab as preventive therapy in patients with CM show significantly reduced frequency of headache and migraine days vs placebo. One-third of patients also reverted to EM. Tools currently used in clinical trials measure the effect of CM on function only during headache days, not before or after. Comorbidity profiles can be used to describe the natural history of migraine; they are not used as treatment outcome measures.

Question type: CME Posttest

Question Introduction (optional ι): A 41-year-old woman with chronic CM reports 21 headache days per month and frequent use of acute headache medication despite previous treatment with 2 preventive therapies.

Question 3: Based on clinical trial data, which of the following might be expected from treatment with a CGRP-targeting mAb?

Answer choices ι:

• Minimal response because of the frequency of her headache days

• Minimal response because of her history of medication overuse

• Good response, with a rapid decrease in frequency of headache days

• Minimal response because of previous failure of 2 preventive therapies to relieve her migraine

Answer explanation: Studies with the CGRP-targeting mAbs erenumab, fremanezumab, eptinezumab, and galcanezumab used as preventive therapy in patients with CM show an early rapid decrease in frequency of headache days, even in patients who failed multiple prior preventive therapies and those with medication overuse.

• Most patients with chronic migraine (CM) and episodic migraine (EM) indicated overall health, stress levels, and enjoyment of free time would be better or a lot better without migraine

• Approximately 20% of those not in a relationship or in a relationship and not living together indicated headaches contributed to relationship problems in the past

• Increasing headache day frequency was associated with increased rates of endorsement that migraine or headaches cause problems

o Held true for all areas of relationships

<<Level 2>> Considerations in Preventive Therapy for Migraine

<<insert Lipton slide 2; Chapter title: Discontinuation of Preventive Therapies; 00:15 >>

<<Level 2>> Patterns of Preventive Therapy Use: IBMS-II[5]

• After starting on oral generic preventive medications for migraine

o After 6 months, only 25% remain on treatment

o After a year, only 15% remain on treatment

• If ask people why they discontinue, they give 2 major reasons independent of what class of medication they are on

o The medicine does not work well enough

o The medicine causes too many side effects

<<insert Lipton slide 3; Chapter title: Treatment Efficacy and Tolerability; 00:54 >>

<<Level 2>> Weighing Treatment Efficacy and Tolerability

• When thinking about whether to continue a patient on preventive treatment, look at 2 × 2 table of efficacy vs side effects

o In cell A, the treatment is working, does not produce side effects

o Very likely the treatment that should be continued

• In cell D, the treatment is not effective and produces side effects

o Very little chance patients will continue treatment and clinicians also unlikely to continue

• Cell C is the most common situation with oral generic preventive medications for migraine

o Medication is at least somewhat effective but also producing side effects

o Patients and clinicians have a difficult choice to make

• Emerging monoclonal antibodies (mAbs) for migraine are particularly attractive

o They provide efficacy with little in the way of penalty in terms of adverse events

o Much greater chance of keeping people in cell A

<<insert Lipton slide 4; 02:13 >>

<<Level 2>> Measuring Migraine Burden in All Phases[6,7]

• Phases of migraine

o In the preheadache (premonitory/prodrome) phase, people may have symptoms or auras

o In the postheadache (postdrome) phase, people may have changes in mood or behavior, fatigue, and scalp sensitivity

<<insert Lipton slide 5; 02:36 >>

<<Level 2>> Measuring Burden of Migraine Before, During, and After Migraine: MPFID[8,9]

• New study data on measuring the burden of migraine during the headache phase, and on the day before and the day after headache

• Migraine Physical Function Impact Diary (MPFID) used to measure disability on headache days and nonheadache days

o Daily diary measure, asks 13 questions about ability to function

<<insert Lipton slide 6; 03:01 >>

<<Level 2>> Burden on Everyday Activities and Physical Impairment by Migraine Phase[9]

• MPFID shows burden on headache days is much greater than on days where no headache is present

• Substantial burden on prodromal and postdromal days

o Previously unappreciated in migraine studies

• In future studies, measure the burden of migraine and treatment benefits on headache days, prodromal days, and postdromal days

o Get a fuller picture both of the burden of disease and the benefits of treatment

<<insert Lipton slide 7; 03:43 >>

<<Level 2>> Identifying Natural Subgroups of Migraine[10]

• Study identifying natural subgroups of migraine to address questions

o Why is migraine so common?

o Why do the clinical features of migraine vary so much from person to person?

o Why is treatment response so variable?

▪ Why does one person respond to a tricyclic antidepressant, another to a beta-blocker, and another to an antiepilepsy drug?

o Why has it been so difficult to identify genes for migraine?

▪ Identified genes for rare forms of migraine like familial hemiplegic migraine

o Most of genetic determinants of more common forms of migraine remain to be identified

• Solution is to do a better job of characterizing natural subgroups of people with migraine

o One approach is to use comorbidity profiles

▪ Use conditions that travel along with migraine to identify natural subgroups of migraine

<<insert Lipton slide 8; 05:15 >>

<<Level 2>> Natural Subgroups of Migraine: Overall Results[10]

• Examined people with migraine and 22 comorbidities

o Discovered 8 natural subgroups

<<insert Lipton slide 9; 05:23 >>

<<Level 2>> Natural Subtype of Migraine: Likelihood of Progression to CM[10]

• Subgroups differed widely in prognosis

o Group with the most comorbidities was most likely to progress to CM

o Group with the fewest comorbidities was least likely to progress

o Substantial differences among groups in prognosis

<<insert Lipton slide 10; 05:47 >>

<<Level 2>> Looking Ahead

• Open questions

o Will these subgroups predict response to treatment?

o Will they correlate with neuroimaging abnormalities found in migraine?

• Important clinical question

o Instead of engaging in a trial-and-error-process for treatment, might be able to get treatment right the first time

<<Level 2>> Migraine Headache: A Treatment Update

<<insert Dodick slide 2; 00:22 >>

<<Level 2>> Lasmiditan for Acute Treatment of Migraine[11,12]

• Two recent updates in acute treatment of migraine

• New 5-hydroxytryptamine (5-HT)1F receptor agonist lasmiditan

o Built on the platform of triptans, which are 5-HT1B, 1D, and to some extent, 5-HT1F receptor agonists

o Because of the 1D receptor agonism, triptans are contraindicated in patients with cardiovascular disease or significant risk factors for cardiovascular disease

• Lasmiditan removed the 1B and the 1D agonism and just left the 1F receptor agonism

o 1F receptors are not located on blood vessels, so there is no cardiovascular liability associated with this medication

• Results of 2 large studies reported: SAMURAI and SPARTAN

o Both large, placebo-controlled, randomized, double-blind studies evaluating efficacy and safety of lasmiditan for acute treatment of migraine

<<insert Dodick slide 3; 01:31 >>

<<Level 2>> Lasmiditan: Phase 3 Study Results[12]

• Used by patients to treat moderate or severe attacks

• Primary endpoint was percentage of patients pain free at 2 hours and percentage of patients free of their most bothersome symptom at 2 hours

o In both studies, 28% to 38% of patients were pain free at 2 hours

o About two-thirds had relief of headache in 2 hours

o More than 40% had relief of the most bothersome symptom

• Both studies showed that all 3 doses tested -- 50, 100, 200 mg -- were all highly statistically superior to placebo

• No serious safety signals

o Dizziness the most common adverse event, reported in about 16% to 18% of patients

• Lasmiditan, if approved, will provide an option for patients for whom triptans are contraindicated

o Option also for patients for whom triptans were not effective or were not tolerated

<<insert Dodick slide 4; 02:35 >>

<<Level 2>> Rimegepant for Acute Treatment of Migraine: Phase 3 Study Results[13,14]

• Studies looked at efficacy of 2 calcitonin gene-related peptide (CGRP)-receptor antagonists

o CGRP is an important neuropeptide in the pathogenesis of migraine

• CGRP receptor antagonists rimegepant and ubrogepant evaluated in large randomized placebo-controlled studies

o Two studies each, or 4 studies in total

• Both CGRP receptor antagonists were shown to be superior to placebo

o Pain-free rates of about 20% for both molecules

o About two-thirds of patients achieved relief of headache at 2 hours

<<insert Dodick slide 5; 03:27 >>

<<Level 2>>Ubrogepant for Acute Treatment of Migraine: Phase 3 Study Results[15,16]

• Percentage of patients able to return to normal function exceeded 40% in studies evaluating these CGRP receptor antagonists

• Like lasmiditan, CGRP receptor antagonists do not constrict blood vessels

o Should have no contraindication for patients with established cardiovascular disease or significant risk factors for cardiovascular disease

• No serious safety signals seen with either drugs in any of the 4 studies

o No serious side effects

o Side effect profile for both compounds look very similar and comparable to placebo

<<insert Dodick slide 6; 04:12 >>

<<Level 2>> CGRP-Targeting mAbs for Migraine Prevention[6,17-26]

• Erenumab, a CGRP-targeting mAb, has been approved by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) for prevention of migraine

• Two drugs are under FDA review right now and a third, an intravenous formulation, is completing phase 3 trials

• All of these mAbs are effective for the preventive treatment of episodic migraine and chronic migraine

<<insert Dodick slide 7; 04:40 >>

<<Level 2>> Early Rapid Onset of CGRP-Targeting mAbs[27-29]

• Effective in reducing the frequency of migraine

• Rapid onset of effect, within about a day or a week

o Unprecedented, for preventive treatments for migraine

<<insert Dodick slide 8; 05:03 >>

<<Level 2>> Benefit of CGRP-Targeting mAbs in Difficult-to-Treat Populations[30-35]

• Effective in patients who were otherwise medically or treatment resistant

• Effective in patients who have failed multiple prophylactic medications

• Effective in patients with medication overuse headache, independent of withdrawal from acute analgesics

• Unique attributes that go far above and beyond just their efficacy in preventing or reducing the frequency of migraine

<<Level 2>> Emerging Approaches for Preventive Therapy of CM

<<insert Halker Singh slide 2; 00:21 >>

<<Level 2>> CM: Features and Challenges[6,36,37]

• CM and EM are clinically, functionally, and anatomically differentiated

o Evidence suggests they may be separate conditions

• Patients with CM usually have more comorbid conditions and more frequent medication overuse

o Complicates clinical management of CM

• Estimated rate of reversion from CM to EM is relatively low

o Studies report remission rates of about 15% to 26%

• Several mAbs targeting CGRP are in development

o The first was recently approved for migraine prevention

<<insert Halker Singh slide 3; Chapter title: Fremanezumab; 01:19 >>

<<Level 2>> Fremanezumab for Prevention of CM: HALO-CM[22]

• Study looked at reversion of patients with CM to EM with fremanezumab treatment

• Fremanezumab is a CGRP mAb that can be given as a monthly or quarterly injection for prevention of migraine

o In clinical trials, shown to significantly reduce frequency of migraine and headache without serious treatment-related adverse events

• Phase 3 HALO-CM study

o Patients aged 18 to 70 years with history of migraine for at least 12 months and who met International Classification of Headache Disorders (ICHD)-3 criteria for CM

o Randomly assigned to fremanezumab given monthly or quarterly or placebo

o Patients kept a daily diary and completed other questionnaires at regular intervals

• Primary endpoint was mean change in the monthly average number of headache days of at least moderate severity at 12 weeks vs baseline

o Secondary endpoints included safety and tolerability measures

• Study met all of its endpoints

<<insert Halker Singh slide 4; 02:42 >>

<<Level 2>> Reversion From CM to EM With Fremanezumab in HALO-CM[38]

• Post hoc analysis of HALO-CM data looked at proportion of patients who reverted from CM to EM

o Met primary outcome

• Fremanezumab demonstrated efficacy as a preventive treatment for CM

o Decrease in mean MHD over the treatment period

• Demonstrated potential benefit for reversion from CM to EM

<<insert Halker Singh slide 5; Chapter title: Eptinezumab 03:28 >>

<<Level 2>> Eptinezumab for Prevention of CM: PROMISE-2[21]

• PROMISE-2 study of the CGRP-targeting mAb eptinezumab as a preventive therapy for CM

o Randomly assigned to a single quarterly infusion of 100 mg or 300 mg dose of eptinezumab or placebo

o Enrolled more than 1000 patients who had approximately 16 monthly migraine days at baseline

• Subjects in both eptinezumab groups had a greater reduction in monthly migraine days vs placebo

• Study also met secondary endpoints

o More than half of subjects achieved >50% reduction in MMD from weeks 1 to 12 and at monthly intervals

o Significantly higher proportion of patients on eptinezumab achieved 100% reduction in monthly migraine days vs placebo
