<<level 2>>VTE: Acute, Long-Term, and Extended Treatment[1]

• The acute phase is the first week after VTE event, and it is the most dangerous time for patients, with the highest risk of death

• The long-term phase is out to the 3-month point, during which data show that all patients require treatment

• At the 3-month point, the extended treatment phase, a number of factors come into play, which determine who requires treatment beyond this point

<<insert slide 4 (01:57) chapter title: Provoked VTE >>

<<level 2>>Provoked VTE[2,3]

• It is challenging to identify the patients who warrant extended treatment

<<insert slide 5 (02:57) chapter title: Unprovoked VTE >>

<<level 2>>Unprovoked VTE[4,5]

• What we have learned is that patients with unprovoked VTE also have a considerable risk of recurrence

• For many clinicians, it is not a driver that prompts continued extended prophylaxis, but in clinical practice, many of these patients have several factors that need to be taken into account

• For these patients, it is much more a challenge to assess the risk of recurrence and to consider extended secondary VTE prevention

<<level 2>> HERDOO2 Rule[2]

• The HERDOO2 rule selects a lower-risk group of women who will not need extended anticoagulation because their risk of recurrence is 3% or less

• Patients at a lower risk of recurrence are patients whose D-dimer is negative at the end of 3 or 6 months of anticoagulation and remains negative on serial testing

<<insert slide 8 (07:02) chapter title: VTE Extension Trials >>

<<level 2>> VTE Extension Trials: NOACs vs Placebo[8-10]

• Risk of recurrence is lower regardless of the non-vitamin K antagonist oral anticoagulant (NOAC) vs placebo

• What is most important is that, even compared with placebo, the bleeding risk is no different with NOACs

<<insert slide 9 (07:43) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>> Extending Anticoagulation[11]

• In the past, patients were not protected due to fear of bleeding and managing VKAs

• NOACs are more convenient and provide safer anticoagulation, so the threshold for protecting patients against recurrent VTE has significantly decreased

• The patient's initial presentation, whether it is a pulmonary embolism (PE) or a deep venous thromboembolism (DVT), is a factor to be taken into account when deciding on extending treatment

• PEs are more likely to recur as PE, and the case-fatality rate of recurrent PE is higher than the potential hazards because of recurrent DVT[12]

<<level 2>>EINSTEIN CHOICE: Study Design[14]

• Patients who received standard anticoagulation for 6 to 12 months were then randomized to 1 of 3 groups: aspirin 100 mg, rivaroxaban 10 mg once daily, or rivaroxaban 20 mg once daily

<<insert slide 13 (13:35) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>>EINSTEIN CHOICE: Primary Outcome -- Recurrent VTE[14]

• Both doses of rivaroxaban were superior to low-dose aspirin

• Low-dose aspirin was not as effective as anticipated

<<insert slide 14 (14:22) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>>EINSTEIN CHOICE: Bleeding Outcomes[14]

• There were similar rates of major bleeding among the 3 groups

• Rivaroxaban 20 mg is associated with a slightly increased risk of nonmajor bleeds, whereas there was no difference between the 10 mg dose and low-dose aspirin

• EINSTEIN CHOICE provides evidence for using the option of rivaroxaban 10 mg for secondary VTE prevention

<<insert slide 15 (15:17) chapter title: >>

<<level 2>> EINSTEIN CHOICE: Provoked, Unprovoked, and History of VTE[14]

• Subgroup analyses showed that these effects were consistent regardless of whether the patient had a provoked VTE, an unprovoked VTE, or a recurrent VTE

• Bleeding risks remain similar among the groups

• Aspirin may be an option only for patients who cannot afford NOACs
