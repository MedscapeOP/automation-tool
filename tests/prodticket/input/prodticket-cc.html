Questions (Evaluations, Assessments, and Posttests)

Question type: CME Posttest

Question Introduction (optional ι):

Question 1: In which of the following patients with active cancer and venous thromboembolism (VTE) could a non-vitamin K antagonist oral anticoagulant (NOAC) be considered for the treatment of VTE?

Answer choices ι:

• A patient with luminal gastrointestinal (GI) cancer

• A patient with breast cancer

• A patient with bladder cancer

• A patient with prostate cancer and esophagitis

Answer explanation: The International Society on Thrombosis and Haemostasis (ISTH) has provided guidance on the use of NOACs for the treatment of cancer-associated VTE. This guidance is based on data from Hokusai VTE-Cancer (edoxaban) and SELECT-D (rivaroxaban) trials. Both trials showed a greater reduction of VTE recurrence with the NOAC vs dalteparin. Major bleeding was, however, greater with the NOAC but was mostly confined to patients with GI cancer. Accordingly, the ISTH suggests that these 2 NOACs may be considered for VTE treatment in patients with cancer who are at low risk of bleeding. In cancers with a high risk of bleeding (luminal GI, genitourinary [GU] tract, and bladder) or patients with cancer who have active GI mucosal abnormalities (duodenal ulcers, gastritis, esophagitis, or colitis), ISTH suggests the use of low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) for VTE treatment. Patients with breast cancer had a low incidence of major bleeding in the two NOAC trials.

Question type: CME Posttest

Question Introduction (optional ι):

Question 2: What is the current recommendation for the primary prevention of VTE in patients with cancer?

Answer choices ι:

• Routine thromboprophylaxis should be considered in all patients with cancer as they are at increased risk of VTE

• Thromboprophylaxis is mandatory in patients with primary brain tumors as they are at high risk of VTE

• Thromboprophylaxis should be considered in high-risk outpatients receiving chemotherapy

• Routine thromboprophylaxis should be considered in all outpatients with cancer who have a low risk of bleeding

Answer explanation: Compared with the general population without cancer, patients with cancer are at increased risk of VTE, but the risk varies considerably across cancer types. Thus, routine thromboprophylaxis is not recommended in unselected, low-risk outpatients with cancer or in patients with high risk of bleeding, such as with primary brain tumors. Conversely, thromboprophylaxis with LMWH should be considered in high-risk outpatients with cancer (Khorana score ≥3 or advanced pancreatic cancer) who are receiving chemotherapy. This recommendation is based on clinical trials that showed a benefit for thromboprophylaxis in the subgroup of patients with high risk of VTE. Clinical trials with NOACs for the prevention of cancer-associated VTE are ongoing. The results of these trials are expected to clarify the role of NAOCs for thromboprophylaxis in patients with cancer.

<<Level 2>> Risk of Cancer-Associated VTE[1-4]

• Risk of venous thromboembolism (VTE) is elevated in cancer

• In general, patients with cancer have a 1:5 chance of developing VTE

• VTE risk is variable and dependent on a number of factors, including

o Cancer type (site of tumor)

o Cancer stage

o Time since diagnosis of cancer

o Cancer treatment

<<insert slide 3: 01:12 >>

<<Level 2>> Risk Factors for Cancer-Associated VTE: Cancer Site[4]

• The predominant risk factor is the location of the primary tumor

• Overall, gastrointestinal (GI), pancreas, brain, lung, and ovarian cancer induce the highest risks

• Relatively lower risks are seen in patients with breast or prostate cancer

<<insert slide 4: 02:27 >>

<<Level 2>> Risk Factors for Cancer-Associated VTE: Cancer Treatment Modality[5]

<<insert slide 5: 04:25 >>

<<Level 2>> Risk Factors for Cancer-Associated VTE: Patient-Related Factors[5]

• Ethnicity

o Risk greater with African American vs Caucasian vs Asian population

o Attributed risk, however, is minor

• Compression of major vessels by tumors

<<insert slide 6; Chapter Title: LMWH for VTE Treatment; 05:18 >>

<<Level 2>> Guideline Recommendations: Cancer-Associated VTE Treatment[6-8]

• Most guidelines recommend monotherapy with low-molecular-weight heparin (LMWH) for at least 6 months

• Latest data from VTE treatment trials with non-vitamin K antagonist oral anticoagulants (NOACs) are challenging the current treatment paradigm

<<insert slide 7: 06:54 >>

<<Level 2>> Challenges With LMWH

• LMWHs are injectable agents

o Injectable agents are treated differently in prescription plans and copayment can be high

o Daily self-injection adds to the burden of treatment

<<insert slide 8: 07:32 >>

<<Level 2>> Persistence With Anticoagulation for Cancer-Associated VTE[9]

• Real-world analysis: LMWH was associated with significantly lower persistence and shorter duration of treatment

<<insert slide 9: 08:33 >>

<<Level 2>> VTE and Major Bleeding Risk in Patients Receiving Chemotherapy: A Real-World Analysis[10]

• Patients with cancer are at high risk of both VTE and major bleeding

• VTE and major bleeding can be fatal

• Major bleeding can be difficult to treat; especially GI mucosal bleeding

• Minor (eg, nuisance) bleeding is also common

<<insert slide 10; Chapter Title: NOACS for VTE Treatment; 09:24 >>

<<Level 2>> Treatment of Cancer-Associated VTE: Clinical Trials of NOACs[11-13]

<<insert slide 11: 10:36 >>

<<Level 2>> Hokusai VTE-Cancer: Design[14]

• Study population with active cancer and symptomatic/incidental deep vein thrombosis (DVT) or proximal pulmonary embolism (PE)

o DVT of proximal lower extremity (popliteal, femoral, or iliac vein thrombosis)

o PE involving segmental or more proximal pulmonary arteries

• All patients were treated for at least 6 months

• Physicians were encouraged to continue treatment for 12 months

<<insert slide 12: 11:45 >>

<<Level 2>> Hokusai VTE-Cancer: Primary Outcome[11]

• Edoxaban was noninferior to dalteparin

<<insert slide 13: 12:05 >>

<<Level 2>> Hokusai VTE-Cancer: Primary Outcome Components[11]

• Rate of recurrent VTE was significantly lower with edoxaban vs dalteparin

• Rate of major bleeding was significantly higher with edoxaban vs dalteparin

• Most major bleeding occurred in patients with GI cancers

<<insert slide 14: 13:11 >>

<<Level 2>> SELECT-D: Design[15]

• Study population similar to Hokusai VTE-Cancer

<<insert slide 15: 13:37 >>

<<Level 2>> SELECT-D: Primary Endpoint – Recurrent VTE at 6 Months[12]

• Rivaroxaban was associated with relatively low VTE recurrence compared with dalteparin

<<insert slide 16: 13:48 >>

<<Level 2>> SELECT-D: Major and CRNM Bleeding at 6 Months[12]

• Rivaroxaban was associated with relatively higher clinically relevant nonmajor (CRNM) bleeding compared with dalteparin

• Most of the major bleeding occurred in patients with GI cancers

<<insert slide 17: 15:01 >>

<<Level 2>> SELECT-D: Major Bleeding by Tumor Type[12]

• Among GI cancers, most major bleeding was confined to upper GI cancers

<<insert slide 18: 15:28 >>

<<Level 2>> Hokusai VTE-Cancer: Major Bleeding by Tumor Type[16]

• Excess major bleeding with edoxaban was confined to patients with GI cancer

<<insert slide 19; Chapter Title: Guidance on Use of NOACs; 16:38 >>

<<Level 2>> NOACs for Treatment of Cancer Associated VTE: Guidance From ISTH[17]

• An estimated 60% to 70% of overall cancer population may be eligible for treatment with NOACs

• In patients with high risk of bleeding, reassess therapy options when bleeding risk changes over time

• Patients with severe thrombocytopenia are candidates for LMWH

o LMWH can be dose-adjusted based on the platelet count

o Fine tuning of dose is not currently possible with NOACs

<<insert slide 20: 18:55 >>

<<Level 2>> Anticoagulation in Cancer Patients With Thrombocytopenia: Guidance From ISTH[18]

• Option to start with one therapy and switch to another when patient condition improves

• For example, in patients with GI cancer

o Could start therapy with LMWH

o If bleeding risk improves, switch to a NOAC

o If platelet count declines after switching to NOAC, bridge to LMWH until platelet count improves to safely resume NOAC

<<insert slide 21; Chapter Title: VTE Prevention; 22:33 >>

<<Level 2>> Guidance for Prevention of Cancer-Associated VTE[19]

• Clinical trials on thromboprophylaxis found benefit in patients with high risk score (Khorana score ≥3)

• Although thromboprophylaxis is recommended in patients with cancer who are at high risk of VTE, it is not usually followed
